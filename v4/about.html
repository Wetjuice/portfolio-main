man mberman — manual page
NAME
mberman
— Game Director, Design Leader, Systems Thinker
SYNOPSIS
mberman --player-first --graybox-first --systems-driven
        --iterate-fast --lead-by-decision --sustainable-design
        --know-your-audience --gameplay-clarity
        [--studio <studio>] [--platform <pc|console|mobile>] [--ship-it]
DESCRIPTION

Michael Berman is a Game Director with 16+ years shipping games across mobile, PC, and console. Currently directing an unannounced AAA title at Sony Interactive Entertainment / Bend Studio.

Specializes in systemic design, player-first thinking, and turning ambiguous creative visions into shippable products. Has led teams from 2 to 15+ designers across first-party Sony, THQ Nordic, and independent studios.

Operates on the principle that good design is invisible — the player should feel smart, not tutorialized.

OPTIONS
--player-first
Build for your audience, not yourself.
▶
Your preferences are a sample size of one. Research, playtesting, and data close the gap between what you think works and what actually does. It’s great when your taste aligns with your players’ — lean on feedback to find where it doesn’t.
--graybox-first
Find the fun before you spend on polish.
▶
There is a time and place for polish, tutorials, and refinement. That time is not first. Keep things as low-fidelity as possible to identify high-confidence features before committing production resources. Polishing the wrong thing is the most expensive mistake in AAA development.
--systems-driven
Design systems, not content.
▶
Content dependency is a debt spiral — you need more content to sustain what you have. Systems-driven design creates experiences that generate themselves. Build the rules that produce the encounters, not the encounters themselves.
--iterate-fast
Test fast. Kill fast. Ship what survives.
▶
The best ideas don’t survive planning meetings — they survive playtests. Run experiments aggressively, kill failures quickly, invest only in what demonstrably works. A strong opinion held loosely beats a weak opinion defended stubbornly.
--lead-by-decision
Leadership is making the hard call, not avoiding it.
▶
The most expensive thing a director can do is leave a decision unmade. Teams wait, work piles up, options narrow. A clear decision — even an imperfect one — is almost always better than prolonged ambiguity. Own the call. Adjust when new information arrives.
--sustainable-design
Design for the long game, not the launch.
▶
Live service taught the industry that launch is the beginning, not the end. Sustainable design means building systems that can grow without exponential resource cost. If a feature requires a full team to maintain after launch, it’s a liability not an asset.
--know-your-audience
A game for everyone is a game for no one.
▶
The instinct to broaden appeal by softening everything results in no clear identity. Know exactly who you’re building for. Make deliberate choices that serve that audience, even when those choices exclude others. Conviction about your player is the foundation of every good design decision.
--gameplay-clarity
If it needs explaining, it needs redesigning.
▶
Players shouldn’t need a tutorial to understand a well-designed mechanic — the mechanic itself communicates its rules. Complexity is a cost, not a feature. Add it deliberately, only once the foundation is clear. When a player is confused, that’s a design problem, not a player problem.
