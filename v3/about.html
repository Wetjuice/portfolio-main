NAME
mberman — Game Director, Design Leader, Systems Thinker
SYNOPSIS
mberman --player-first --graybox-first --systems-driven
--iterate-fast --lead-by-decision --sustainable-design
--know-your-audience --gameplay-clarity
[--studio <studio>] [--platform <pc|console|mobile>] [--ship-it]
DESCRIPTION
Michael Berman is a Game Director and Design Leader with 16+ years shipping games across mobile, PC, and console. Currently directing an unannounced AAA title at Sony Interactive Entertainment / Bend Studio.
Specializes in systemic design, player-first thinking, and the unglamorous but essential work of turning ambiguous creative visions into shippable products. Has led teams from 2 to 15+ designers across first-party Sony, THQ Nordic, and independent studios.
Operates on the principle that good design is invisible — the player should feel smart, not tutorialized; engaged, not manipulated; challenged, not frustrated.
OPTIONS / PRINCIPLES
--player-first
Build for your audience, not yourself.
Player empathy over designer taste. Research closes the gap.
01
Your preferences are a sample size of one. The target audience is not you. Research, playtesting, and data close the gap between what you think works and what actually does. It's great when your taste aligns with your players' — lean on feedback to find where it doesn't.
--graybox-first
Find the fun before you spend on polish.
Low-fidelity prototyping identifies high-confidence features early.
02
There is a time and place for polish, tutorials, onboarding, and refinement. That time is not first. Keep things as low-fidelity as possible to identify high-confidence features before committing production resources. Polishing the wrong thing is the most expensive mistake in AAA development.
--systems-driven
Design systems, not content.
Content requires more content to sustain itself. Systems compound.
03
Content dependency is a debt spiral — you need more content to sustain the content you already have. Systems-driven design creates experiences that generate themselves. Build the rules that produce the encounters, not the encounters themselves. Emergent gameplay is sustainable gameplay.
--iterate-fast
Test fast. Kill fast. Ship what survives.
Ideas are cheap. Validated ideas are valuable.
04
The best ideas don't survive planning meetings — they survive playtests. Run experiments aggressively, kill failures quickly, and invest only in what demonstrably works. A strong opinion held loosely is more valuable than a weak opinion defended stubbornly.
--lead-by-decision
Leadership is making the hard call, not avoiding it.
Ambiguity is the enemy. Decisive direction unblocks teams.
05
The most expensive thing a director can do is leave a decision unmade. Teams wait, work piles up, and options narrow. A clear decision — even an imperfect one — is almost always better than prolonged ambiguity. Own the call. Adjust when new information arrives.
--sustainable-design
Design for the long game, not the launch.
Features that require constant feeding are technical debt.
06
Live service has taught the industry that launch is the beginning, not the end. Sustainable design means building systems that can grow without exponential resource cost. If a feature requires a full team to maintain after launch, it's a liability not an asset.
--know-your-audience
A game for everyone is a game for no one.
Clarity of audience is clarity of design.
07
The instinct to broaden appeal by softening everything results in a product with no clear identity. Know exactly who you're building for. Make deliberate choices that serve that audience, even when those choices exclude others. Conviction about your player is the foundation of every good design decision.
--gameplay-clarity
If it needs explaining, it needs redesigning.
Clarity is the first feature. Complexity is earned.
08
Players shouldn't need a tutorial to understand a well-designed mechanic — the mechanic itself should communicate its rules. Complexity is a cost, not a feature. Add it deliberately, only once the foundation is clear. When a player is confused, that's a design problem, not a player problem.
