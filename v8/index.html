PERSONNEL FILE

I've spent the better part of fourteen years learning how to make strangers feel something through a screen. It started at a twelve-person indie studio in Vancouver, where I discovered that a perfectly tuned jump arc could make someone laugh out loud, and a well-placed environmental detail could make them stop breathing for a full second.

From there, the trajectory went exactly where restless curiosity takes you — through the trenches of AAA production at Obsidian Forge Interactive, into the experimental crucible of Parallax Studios' narrative division, and eventually to my current role leading combat and systems design at Aether Dynamics.

My work lives at the intersection of systemic depth and emotional resonance. I believe the best game design is invisible — that players should feel like authors of their own experience, not passengers on a scripted ride. Every system I build serves a story. Every mechanic is a verb the player uses to speak.

DESIGN DIRECTIVE

"The best games don't tell you what to feel. They build a space where feeling becomes inevitable."

I

Systems Are Stories

Every mechanic is a sentence in a language the player is learning to speak. The best design creates grammar — consistent, learnable rules that reward mastery and punish assumptions. When your systems tell a story, your narrative doesn't have to carry the entire weight.

II

Friction Is A Feature

Not all resistance is bad design. The right kind of friction creates tension, forces decision-making, and makes eventual triumph transcendent. The wrong kind creates frustration. Knowing the difference is the entire job.

III

Design For The Verb

Players don't remember features — they remember actions. "I climbed," "I chose," "I survived." Every system I build starts with the verb. What will the player do? How will it feel? Everything else is architecture in service of that moment.

IV

Invisible Elegance

The highest compliment to a designer is when no one notices the design. If players feel like authors of their own experience — not passengers on your ride — you've done it right. Ego has no place in systems design. Only clarity.
